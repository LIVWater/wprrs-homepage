Match day
Home/Resources/Match Day Duties

Prepare like a professional.

Players spend the week getting fit and devising strategy to beat their opposition. The least the referee can do is to prepare with the same dedication.

The referee's match day starts long before kick-off and ends well after the final whistle.

Match day duties cover the full arc — preparation in the week leading in, a structured pre-match routine at the venue, controlled in-game communication and safety management, and the post-match reporting that protects the integrity of every fixture. The same protocol applies whether you're refereeing a Beginners match or Division 1.

Before the match

Preparation starts on Monday.

The work that makes Saturday calm starts long before the warm-up. Cover the basics every week and there are no surprises.

01

Confirm the appointment

Acknowledge the appointment in WHISTLE. Confirm kick-off time, venue, competition, and any specific protocols (junior playing times, school regulations, festival rules).

02

Review the laws

Refresh the current World Rugby Laws and any local regulations that apply — primary school variations, schools' Code of Conduct, and the playing-time rules for the level you're officiating.

03

Equipment check

Whistle, two spares, watch (set and tested), cards, notebook and pen, water bottle, kit (jersey, shorts, socks, boots), studs checked, hi-vis and warmer for assistant duties.

04

Arrive 60 minutes before kick-off

Find your changing space, meet the home liaison, walk the field for hazards, and check goalposts, line markings, and the surrounds for safety. Earlier for festivals or unfamiliar venues.

05

Pre-match briefing

Meet both captains 15 minutes before kick-off. Cover scrum engagement, tackle area expectations, foul play tolerance, communication protocol, and the BokSmart Blue Card procedure for suspected concussion.

During the match

Lead with consistency.

Consistency is the only thing the players, coaches, and assistants will remember. Lead the contact area, communicate clearly, manage the tempo, and protect the players above everything else.

01
Player welfare first

Suspected concussion is a Blue Card and an immediate exit. No exceptions, no negotiation, no second opinion required on the field.

02
Positioning

Stay close enough to see, far enough not to interfere. Anticipate the next phase rather than chase the last one.

03
Clear communication

Short, audible commands at the breakdown. Signals are crisp and held long enough to be read by the assistants and the scorer.

04
Manage the tempo

Use advantage. Keep the game flowing. Avoid over-officiating — let the players play unless safety or law clearly says otherwise.

05
Card protocol

Yellow for deliberate technical infringement and reckless foul play. Red for dangerous play and the second yellow. Note the time, the number, and the offence.

06
Use your team

Assistants are your eyes off the ball. Trust them on touch, foul play behind play, and offside lines. Acknowledge their signal openly.

After the match

The whistle is not the end.

What happens in the 60 minutes after full-time often matters more than the 80 minutes before it. Get this right and the Society works as it should.

01

Signal full-time clearly

Stop your watch, signal full-time with the long whistle, confirm the score with the time-keeper and both teams. Shake hands with players and assistants on the field.

02

Be present in the clubhouse

Join the post-match function. Brief, polite, professional. Decline to debrief individual decisions with coaches or players — that conversation happens through the match-review process.

03

File the match report

Submit your match report in WHISTLE on the same day. Include the score, cards issued (number, time, offence), injuries reported, and any incidents that require Society follow-up.

Submit a match report →

04

Incident reporting

If a serious incident occurred — physical assault, sustained verbal abuse, dangerous foul play that requires citing — submit the dedicated Incident Report. The Society cannot act on what it does not see.

05

Reflect

Note three things that went well and one thing to work on. Take this into your next regional session — your coach will use it as the starting point for review.
