Experimental law changes — what applies.

World Rugby's community-rugby review has introduced experimental law variations at grassroots level. A summary of what's changed and what active members need to be ready for.

As part of World Rugby's community-rugby review, several experimental law variations have been introduced at grassroots level. These are designed to improve player welfare, simplify decision-making for referees, and accelerate the flow of community matches.

The variations apply to all sanctioned community fixtures across Western Province for the upcoming season. Higher-level competitions — provincial cup, Currie Cup First Division, Varsity Cup — continue to apply the full World Rugby Law Book without these variations.

If in doubt about whether a variation applies, default to the standard Law Book. The variations are designed to be permissive, not punitive.

The headline variations

1. Tackle height

The tackle-height variation introduced last season remains in force. The tackler must make initial contact at or below the base of the sternum. This lowers the threshold for high-tackle sanction at community level and aligns with the broader player-welfare agenda.

Referees should default to the World Rugby Head Contact Process. The three-step framework — danger of high degree, mitigating factors, sanction — applies as written.

2. Pre-bound scrum

At community level, front-row binding may be set before the engagement call. This reduces collapses and speeds up scrum reset. The "crouch, bind, set" sequence is replaced with "crouch, set" at affiliated junior and 4th–5th XV fixtures.

3. 50:22 kick — clarification

The 50:22 kick remains in the Laws but the application guidance has been clarified. The kick must be from inside the kicker's own half (not on the half-way line), and must bounce before going into touch. The receiving team throws in.

4. Goal-line drop-out

When a defender carries the ball into their own in-goal and grounds it, play restarts with a goal-line drop-out — not a 5-metre scrum to the attacking team. This applies at all community levels.

5. Sin-bin reductions for technical offences

At Beginner through Division 3 level, repeat technical infringement may be sanctioned by a 5-minute sin-bin rather than the standard 10. The aim is proportionate sanction without rendering community matches uncompetitive.

What we need from members

Three actions before the season opens:

  • Re-read the SA Rugby Law Book and the World Rugby variation summary distributed to members
  • Attend the law clarification session at the next virtual meeting (16 September, 19:30 SAST)
  • Apply the variations consistently — write a brief note in your match report when a variation directly influenced an outcome, so the Education & Training portfolio can track application across the Province

Where to read more

The official World Rugby explainer is available at laws.worldrugby.org. The SA Rugby Law Book — including local regulations and the schools' Code of Conduct — is published on the SA Referees site.

If you have questions about specific scenarios you encountered last season that you think the variations would have affected, bring them to the next virtual meeting or email the Education & Training portfolio holder, Mornay Colville.
